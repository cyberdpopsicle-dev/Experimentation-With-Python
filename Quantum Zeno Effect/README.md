## The Quantum Zeno Effect: Freezing Quantum Dynamics Through Measurement

In quantum mechanics, the act of measurement is not a passive observation. It is an active intervention that fundamentally alters the state of the system. This leads to one of the most counterintuitive phenomena in physics: the Quantum Zeno Effect.
The premise is straightforward: a quantum system can be prevented from evolving simply by measuring it frequently enough. 
In this simulation, we model a two-level system (a qubit) driven by a coherent field. Under ideal conditions, the qubit undergoes clean Rabi oscillations, continuously flipping between its ground state and its excited state. 
However, we can model interaction with a measurement apparatus by solving the Lindblad Master Equation with a dephasing collapse operator. This operator continuously destroys phase coherence, effectively acting as a non-stop, weak measurement of the state.

---
# The results reveal three distinct regimes:

1. Unmeasured: The state oscillates smoothly between ground and excited populations.
2. Weakly Measured: Coherence begins to degrade, leading to heavily damped oscillations.
3. Strongly Measured (The Zeno Regime): The coherent transition is almost entirely suppressed. The qubit remains locked in its initial ground state, frozen in place by the strength of the measurement coupling.

*This transition from coherent rotation to frozen stability highlights how environmental coupling and measurement can be engineered to trap and protect fragile quantum states from unwanted transitions.*
